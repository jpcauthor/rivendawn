The Plateau

For over two hundred years, Rivendawn basked in the warmth of the Citadel Plateau, an era of unprecedented peace and scholarly advancement. The great regions matured into stable societies while the Citadel reached the zenith of its influence, serving as a neutral judge and the foremost guardian of the Song.

Trade flowed like water through the riverlands, and scholars from every corner of the realm filled the archives with a clarity of record never seen before. Yet, beneath this veneer of prosperity, subtle frictions and bureaucratic debates began to fray the edges of unity.

VI. The Citadel Plateau (650–860 RC)

The centuries between 650 RC and 860 RC are commonly referred to by Citadel historians as The Citadel Plateau. This era represents one of the longest sustained periods of relative stability in the recorded history of Rivendawn.

By the beginning of this period, the foundations of civilization had firmly taken root. The Citadel continued to function as the central institution of scholarship and record keeping, while the three emerging regions—The Forge, The Cradle, and Limestone—had begun to establish stable societies with distinct cultural identities.

Although differences in philosophy and governance existed among these regions, open conflict remained uncommon for much of the Plateau period. Trade networks expanded gradually, communication between distant settlements improved, and the movement of travelers, merchants, and scholars became more frequent than in earlier centuries.

During this era, the Citadel’s influence reached its greatest extent as a neutral institution respected by many cultures. Scholars from across Rivendawn traveled to study within its halls, contributing to the expansion of its archives and the development of numerous fields of study. Historical record keeping improved significantly, allowing historians to document events with greater clarity and consistency than in previous eras.

At the same time, the study of Resonance continued to develop. While the phenomenon remained complex and often unpredictable, generations of Citadel scholars refined the methods by which Resonant abilities could be observed, categorized, and trained. The Citadel gradually became recognized not only as a center of historical learning but also as the foremost institution dedicated to the responsible study of the Song and its influence upon the world.

Despite these advances, the Citadel Plateau was not without its tensions. As the regions matured, differences in political philosophy and cultural priorities occasionally created friction between them. The Forge emphasized discipline and strength, the Cradle increasingly favored governance and diplomacy, and Limestone maintained its traditions of communal resilience and independence. While these differences rarely escalated into large-scale conflict, they shaped the political landscape of Rivendawn in subtle but lasting ways.

Within the Citadel itself, the expanding bureaucracy of scholars, librarians, and archivists created new challenges. As the institution grew, so too did debates concerning the management of knowledge, the authority of the Citadel’s leadership, and the appropriate role of scholars in the affairs of the wider world. These debates did not immediately threaten the Citadel’s stability, but they revealed underlying divisions that would later prove significant.

For nearly two centuries, however, the Plateau period endured as an age of gradual growth and cautious balance. Civilizations strengthened their institutions, cultures deepened their traditions, and the archives of the Citadel expanded to become the most comprehensive collection of knowledge in Rivendawn.

This long stability eventually came to an abrupt end during the ninth century of the Resonant Calendar. A series of events within the Citadel itself would fracture the institution that had guided Rivendawn for generations.

These events mark the beginning of the Citadel Schism, one of the most consequential periods in the modern history of the world.
