The Age of Isolation

For three centuries, the lands of Rivendawn grew in solitary splendor, separated by treacherous peaks and ancient forests. In this Age of Isolation, every valley and mountain range fostered its own unique culture, each community a world unto itself.

The Citadel remained the intellectual heart, yet its scribes were rare travelers in a realm where long-distance journeys were perilous and few dared to cross the borders of their own home. It was during this time that the Song began to resonate more strongly in the blood of individuals, drawing the gifted to the Citadel to sharpen their unusual abilities.

From this quiet independence, the first true centers of power began to stir. The paths between the hidden kingdoms are waiting to be walked once more.

Uncover the secrets of the era when every horizon held a new mystery.

The Age of Isolation (300–600 RC)

The period between 300 RC and 600 RC is known to Citadel historians as the Age of Isolation. During these centuries, the peoples of Rivendawn continued to develop their cultures and territories largely independent of one another, with limited sustained contact between distant regions.

Although the Citadel had succeeded in restoring writing and preserving knowledge, its influence during this period remained primarily intellectual rather than political. The institution served as a center of scholarship and record keeping, but it did not attempt to govern the lands beyond its walls. As a result, the wider world evolved through the independent efforts of numerous communities whose priorities centered on survival, local governance, and the establishment of stable homelands.

Across Rivendawn, settlements gradually expanded into recognizable territories associated with particular cultures and lineages. Forest regions became the domain of communities closely tied to woodland environments, while mountainous territories fostered societies adapted to stone, metal, and subterranean life. Open plains and fertile valleys gave rise to agricultural settlements whose stability depended upon seasonal rhythms and the careful management of land and water.

Despite these developments, travel between regions remained rare and often dangerous. Harsh terrain, uncertain weather, and the presence of hostile creatures or raiding bands made long-distance journeys difficult. Many communities therefore developed strong internal identities and traditions while maintaining little knowledge of the wider world beyond their immediate surroundings.

The Citadel attempted to maintain contact with distant settlements through occasional expeditions of scribes and scholars. These journeys produced many of the historical records that survive from this era, though the information gathered was often fragmentary and separated by long intervals of time. For this reason, the historical record of the Age of Isolation is uneven, with certain regions better documented than others.

It was also during this period that Resonance began to appear more consistently among individuals across Rivendawn. While the phenomenon remained poorly understood, the Citadel’s scholars continued to record observations and refine early theories concerning the Song and its influence upon the world. Students capable of perceiving or shaping Resonance were increasingly drawn to the Citadel for training, strengthening the institution’s role as a center for the study of these unusual abilities.

By the later centuries of the Age of Isolation, the cumulative growth of settlements had begun to change the structure of society across Rivendawn. Trade routes slowly emerged between neighboring communities, agricultural regions expanded their production, and certain settlements began to assume greater regional importance.

These developments set the stage for the emergence of the first true centers of political and economic power beyond the Citadel itself. By the beginning of the seventh century of the Resonant Calendar, several regions had grown sufficiently stable to support the rise of permanent cities and organized systems of governance.

The appearance of these early urban centers marks the beginning of the next major period in Rivendawn’s recorded history: the Age of City-States.
