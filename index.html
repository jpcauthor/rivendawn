The Present Age

A World at the Precipice

From the Citadel Archives — Department of Historical Studies

The world of Rivendawn now stands in a time historians increasingly refer to as the Present Age. Unlike the earlier centuries following the founding of the Citadel — when civilization struggled simply to survive and rebuild — this era is defined by growing complexity, rising political tension, and a widening uncertainty about the future.

For nearly eight centuries after the establishment of the first Citadel, the world experienced gradual recovery. Writing returned. Trade routes expanded. Settlements grew into organized regions capable of sustaining stable populations. Institutions dedicated to the study of Resonance brought new understanding to forces that had once been feared as mysterious or uncontrollable.

Yet stability has never meant permanence.

The conflict now commonly referred to as the Citadel Schism has altered the balance of power across Rivendawn. The Crimson Citadel, once the primary guardian of history and scholarship, has grown increasingly authoritarian under the Dominus Scientiae, Grevon. Opposing this is the newly established Azure Citadel — formed by those who rejected the growing corruption, still young, still fragile, still defining itself.

Beyond the Citadels, the regions of Rivendawn have matured into powerful societies in their own right. The disciplined industrial strength of the Forge. The structured governance and scholarship of the Cradle. The resilient communal traditions of Limestone. Each represents a distinct answer to the question every civilization must eventually face.

Complicating this landscape are reports of increasingly unstable Resonant phenomena, the lingering threat of Shadowmancy, and the political consequences of the Citadel's internal fracture.

And that decision remains far from settled.

What Is Rivendawn?

The world you've inherited

▼

Rivendawn is what endured after the Great Malleability — a cataclysm so complete that the world had to rebuild itself from memory and mud. The name itself is a declaration: a new dawn, born from a world that was riven open and left to remember the wound.

Three regional powers now claim the land. Two Citadels stand apart from them. And beneath everything, the Song hums — indifferent to all of it.

"The world did not ask for heroes. It simply ran out of alternatives."

The Rivens

Wounds that never healed

▼

Rivens are tears in the fabric of the world — toxic, expanding, and utterly hostile to life. They melt flesh, poison water, and leave generations twisted by proximity. The land around them withers. Animals near them go hollow-eyed and frenzied.

Rivendawn takes its name from them. Every scar on the map has a Riven at its source.

ExpandingToxicLinked to Shadowmancy

"If the air smells ancient and diseased — turn back. Nothing that enters a Riven comes back unchanged."

The Song

What powers the world

▼

The Song is the underlying harmonic lattice of reality. It moves through stone, water, flesh, and memory. It was present before the first Citadel was built. It will persist after the last one falls.

Those who learn to align with it — Resonant practitioners — can instruct the Song to act. They don't cast spells. They issue structured will, and the Song obeys. Within law.

The Year is 877 RC

Where you are in history

▼

RC marks the founding of the Crimson Citadel — humanity's first organized attempt to remember itself. Everything before that is pre-history, fragments, and guesswork.

In 877 RC, the three regions are in uneasy balance. The Crimson Citadel has fallen to corruption. The Azure Citadel is young and hidden. And something is moving in the dark that none of the factions are prepared for.

"A number that pretends at order while measuring a history anything but."

"Three regions. Each convinced the world would collapse without them. It has not — which is, frankly, to the world's credit rather than theirs."

Limestone

The Southern Bedrock

▼

Limestone doesn't seek power. It endures. Born from survival pacts and shared burden, its people are stoic, earthy, and fiercely loyal. They govern through a Council of Stewards chosen by reputation — no crowns, no thrones.

Their justice system erases wrongdoers from public memory entirely. In Limestone, oblivion is worse than death.

"The foundation stone everyone forgets — until it cracks."

The Forge

The Grey Fortress

▼

The Forge is a machine built for war, ruled absolutely by the Iron Widow — a woman who chose her own title because tragedy wasn't fast enough. Love is weakness. Obedience is survival. Pain proves worth.

Children are subjected to brutal mountain trials. Adults are sorted by utility. Worth is determined by usefulness, not birth.

Military-IndustrialCaste HierarchyMost Dangerous

"There is no cruelty. There is only process."

The Cradle

The First City

▼

The Cradle leads without kings and governs without rulers. Born from observation and iteration rather than conquest, its people are highly literate, strategically minded, and merciless toward tyrants.

Its military is intentionally small and frighteningly effective. Its greatest weapon is not its army — it's its ability to shape a future fewer people must fear.

"They are insufferable. And effective."

How They Relate

The balance that shouldn't work but does

▼

The Forge attacked Limestone once. Limestone bled but refused to fall. The Cradle intervened with twelve squads, six days, and no armies — dismantling the entire invasion through misinformation and sabotage. The Forge named it The Shame of the Iron Widow and did not forget.

The Forge's campaign against the Cradle ended when Grey left a note on the Iron Widow's pillow, written in her top lieutenant's blood: "I suggest you withdraw from Limestone. — Greighton." She did.

"Three parasites living within Rivendawn, each convinced that what they take makes them virtuous. They are all the same."

"One has endured for centuries. The other is barely a decade old. Both matter more than either would admit."

The Crimson Citadel

Founded 0 RC — Corrupted by 860 RC

▼

The first institution to emerge after the Great Malleability. Its purpose was to preserve truth, restore literacy, and serve as the neutral anchor of civilization. For centuries, it did exactly that.

Then Grevon — the Dominus — rose through manipulation rather than merit, allowed Shadowmancy to root itself in the Citadel, and hollowed it from the inside. It is now outwardly powerful and inwardly rotten.

The Azure Citadel

Founded 867 RC — Emerging 877 RC

▼

Born from betrayal. When Grevon purged the Vanguard after Greighton killed the corrupt Lorn Zyreth, thirty-five survivors fled under Adella Vael'Astra and founded something new — a sanctuary for truth, uncorrupted by shadow.

Led by Librarian Veysha, it seeks not dominion but restoration: free the imprisoned, expose the corruption, rebuild what the Crimson once claimed to be.

The First Librarian — The Reason Any of This Exists

▼

Perhaps the most powerful mind of his age. Certainly the most self-congratulatory. He founded the Crimson Citadel, refused worship with equal parts arrogance and accuracy, and five centuries ago obliterated an army of Shadowcasters in what history calls Folly's War.

He left one alive as a warning. That survivor was Grevon's ancestor. That mercy became the Citadel's doom.

How Resonance Works

You don't cast. You instruct.

▼

Resonance is the structured instruction of the Song. You align your will with a Path — a governing harmonic — and issue a precise instruction. The Song obeys. Within law.

Every practitioner carries a Wellspring — a finite reservoir. Spend it carelessly and it empties. When it empties, the connection collapses. This is not punishment. It is preservation.

Shadowmancy

The unlawful path — do not.

▼

Shadowmancy is not a Path. It is a failure state. It does not harmonize with the Song — it negates it. It extracts without renewal. It destabilizes what it touches.

Those drawn to it often believe themselves exceptional. History records them as missing.

What Resonance Cannot Do

The laws are not suggestions

▼

Vitae cannot kill. Aeris cannot anchor. Terra cannot flow. Ignis cannot preserve. Aqua cannot erase what it absorbs. Obtenebrate cannot dominate through open force.

Resonance accelerates what is already possible. It does not create impossibility.

"Attempt to bend a First Law and the Song resists you — not because it judges, but because it was never designed to do what you are asking."

"You have been paying attention. I can tell because you are still here. That either speaks well of you — or poorly of your survival instincts."

Names to Remember

History was shaped by these hands

▼

Greighton (Grey) — Former Captain of the Vanguard's Redoubt. Shadow Hunter. The most dangerous man in the realm and the one most likely to be standing between you and something worse.

Ranessa Barkroot — Arch Druid's daughter. Warrior. The woman who has routinely stymied the Forge's campaigns through sheer refusal to be stopped.

Grevon — the Dominus — The despot of the Crimson Citadel. The man who took mercy as an invitation.

Sune — A young woman who woke barefoot in an abandoned farm and does not yet know what she is. The world's balance currently depends on her.

What No One Will Tell You

Read this carefully

▼

The three regions are not your friends. They are your context. They will use you, reward you, and abandon you in roughly that order.

The Song does not reward virtue. It does not punish corruption. It reveals. When you reach for it in rage, your instability magnifies. When you reach for it in clarity, it answers cleanly.

"It is not foolish to wish to be a hero. It is, however, catastrophically foolish to fail at being one."
